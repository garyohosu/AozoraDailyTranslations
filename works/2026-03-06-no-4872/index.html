"Impressions of Favorite Books" is a 1920 autobiographical essay by Ryunosuke Akutagawa. He looks back on the books he loved from childhood through adulthood, and reflects on how his literary taste shifted over time. (QA warning)

Ryunosuke Akutagawa: Impressions of Favorite Books Impressions of Favorite Books Ryunosuke Akutagawa When I was a child, the book I loved most was Journey to the West. I still love it today. As an allegorical work, I doubt there is anything in the West that can compare. Even Bunyan’s famous The Pilgrim’s Progress is no match for Journey to the West. Another of my favorites was Water Margin, which I still read with pleasure even now. At one point I had all the names of its 108 heroes memorized by heart. Even then, books like Water Margin and Journey to the West were far more interesting to me than Shunro Oshikawa’s adventure novels and the like. Even before entering middle school, I was devoted to Tokutomi Roka’s Nature and Life, Chogyu’s Miscellaneous Thoughts on the Heike, and Kojima Usui’s On Japanese Landscapes. At the same time, I also loved Natsume’s I Am a Cat, Kyoka’s Furyusen, and Ryuu’s Arare-zake. So I can’t laugh at other people. I too had a phase like the one described in the Young Men of Letters section of Bungei Club: “Tolstoy, Tsubouchi, Omachi Keigetsu.” After graduating from middle school I read all kinds of books, though there were no particular ones I repeatedly returned to. Generally speaking, I liked dazzling novels by writers like Wilde and Gautier. That probably came from my temperament, but I think it was also definitely a reaction against my boredom with Japanese naturalist fiction. Around the time I finished higher school, however, my tastes and my whole way of seeing things took a sharp turn, and I came to dislike writers like Wilde and Gautier intensely. It was around then that I became devoted to Strindberg. In my state of mind at the time, any art that lacked a Michelangelo-like force felt like rubble to me. I suppose this was influenced by Jean-Christophe, which I was reading then. That state of mind continued even after I graduated from university, but my worship of blazing power gradually faded, and for about the past year I have found myself most drawn to books with quiet strength. Even so, by “quiet” I do not mean merely calm; works without strength do not interest me much. In this respect, novels by Stendhal, Merimee, and in Japanese literature Saikaku, are books I now find both enjoyable and useful. As an aside, I recently took out Jean-Christophe and reread it, but it did not move me as deeply as it once did. I wondered whether the books of that period had simply gone stale for me, but when I opened Anna Karenina and read two or three chapters, it still felt as precious as before.
