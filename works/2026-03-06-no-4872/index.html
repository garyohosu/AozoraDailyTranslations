Aozora Work No.4872

by Author No.4872 — 2026-03-06

In this autobiographical literary reflection, the narrator traces how his reading tastes evolved from childhood fascination with Chinese classics to a mature preference for works marked by quiet but enduring power.

My favorite book in childhood was Journey to the West, above all. Even now, it remains one of my favorites. As an allegorical tale, I doubt the West has produced a single masterpiece to equal it. Even Bunyan’s famous Pilgrim’s Progress is no match for Journey to the West. Then there was Water Margin, another favorite of mine, and I still love it to this day. At one point I had memorized the names of all one hundred and eight heroes in it. Even then, I found Water Margin and Journey to the West far more interesting than Oshikawa Shunro’s adventure novels and the like. Even before entering middle school, I was devoted to Tokutomi Roka’s Nature and Life, Chogyu’s Reflections on the Tale of the Heike, and Kojima Usui’s On Japanese Landscapes. At the same time, I read Natsume’s I Am a Cat, Kyoka’s Furyusen, and Ryokuu’s Arare-zake with equal enthusiasm. So I can hardly laugh at other people: I too had that phase, like the one in Bungei Club’s “Roster of Young Men of Letters,” of “Tolstoy, Tsubouchi Shiko, and Omachi Keigetsu.” After finishing middle school I read all kinds of books, though I can’t point to any particular favorites. Generally speaking, I liked dazzling, ornate novels by writers such as Wilde and Gautier. That probably came from my temperament, but it was also surely a reaction against growing tired of Japanese naturalist fiction. Yet around the time I graduated from higher school, my tastes and way of seeing things took a sharp turn, and I came to dislike writers like Wilde and Gautier intensely. This was when I became deeply drawn to Strindberg. In the state of mind I had then, any art that lacked a Michelangelo-like force felt like rubble. I suspect that was the influence of books I was reading then, such as Jean-Christophe. That mood lasted even after I graduated from university, but my worship of blazing power gradually faded, and for about the last year I have been most attracted to books with a quiet strength. Even so, by “quiet” I do not mean merely placid; works that are quiet but powerless do not interest me much. In this respect, novels by Stendhal, Mérimée, and in Japan Saikaku are, for me now, both enjoyable and instructive. As an aside, I recently took out Jean-Christophe and tried rereading it, but it did not move me as it once did. I wondered whether books from that period were no good after all, but when I opened Anna Karenina and read a few chapters, it felt as precious as ever.
