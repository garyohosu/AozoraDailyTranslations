The God Agni

by Akutagawa, Ryunosuke · 2026-03-08

This excerpt from Ryunosuke Akutagawa's "The God Agni" unfolds in Shanghai, where an old Indian fortune-teller claims to receive prophecies from the fire god Agni. A Japanese youth named Endo suspects she is hiding the missing daughter of a Japanese consul, and his confrontation with her turns into a terrifying display of sorcery. (QA warning)

I

In a certain district of Shanghai, China, on the second floor of a house so dim it was dark even at noon, a sinister-looking old Indian woman was talking intently with an American man who seemed to be a merchant.

"The truth is, I've come again to ask for a fortune," said the American,

and lit a fresh cigarette.

"A fortune? I've decided not to tell fortunes for a while."

With a mocking look, the old woman glared at his face.

"Lately, even when I go to the trouble of reading for people, too many of them won't even give a proper fee."

"Of course I'll pay."

Without a trace of reluctance, the American tossed a three-hundred-dollar check in front of her.

"Take this for now. If your fortune comes true, I'll reward you separately then."

At the sight of the three-hundred-dollar check, the old woman suddenly became all smiles.

"This is so much, I almost feel bad taking it. So then, what exactly do you want me to divine this time?"

"What I want to know is..."

With the cigarette still between his lips, the American gave a sly smile.

"Exactly when a war between Japan and the United States will happen. If we know that for certain, merchants like us can make a fortune in no time."

"Then come tomorrow. I'll have divined it by then."

"All right. Just make sure there's no mistake."

The old Indian woman thrust out her chest proudly.

"In fifty years, my fortunes have never missed once. After all, it is the god Agni himself who gives the oracle."

After the American left, the old woman went to the doorway of the next room and called out,

"Keiren! Keiren!"

The one who came in answer was a beautiful Chinese girl. Yet perhaps because of some hardship, her full cheeks were pale as wax.

"What are you dawdling for? Honestly, there never was such a shameless girl as you. You were surely dozing off in the kitchen again, weren't you?"

No matter how much she was scolded, Keiren kept her head lowered and said nothing.

"Listen carefully. Tonight, for the first time in a long while, we'll consult the god Agni. Be ready for that."

The girl raised sorrowful eyes to the old woman's pitch-dark face.

"Tonight?"

"Tonight at midnight. Understand? Don't forget."

The old Indian woman raised a finger as if threatening her.

"If you make me go to trouble again like last time, then this time your life is finished. If I wanted to kill the likes of you, it'd be easier than wringing a chick's neck..."

As she said this, the old woman's face suddenly tightened. Noticing her opponent, she looked and saw that Keiren had gone to the window and was gazing out at the lonely street through the open glass pane.

"What are you looking at?"

Keiren turned even paler and looked up at the old woman's face once more.

"Fine, fine. If you're going to make a fool of me like that, then I suppose you still haven't suffered enough."

With angry eyes, the old woman raised the broom that was there.

At that exact instant, someone seemed to have come outside, and the sound of pounding on the door suddenly rang out harshly.

II

At about that same time that day, a young Japanese man happened to pass in front of this house. For whatever reason, the moment he caught sight of the Chinese girl looking out from the upstairs window, he stood rooted there in stunned amazement.

Then an older Chinese rickshaw puller happened to pass by as well.

"Hey, hey. Do you know who lives on that second floor?"

The Japanese man suddenly asked him. Still gripping the rickshaw handles, the Chinese man looked up at the high second floor.

"There? An old Indian woman, I think, lives there," he answered uneasily, and seemed about to move on.

"Wait a moment. And what sort of trade does that old woman do?"

"A fortune-teller. But rumor around here says she even uses magic. If you value your life, you'd better stay away from her place."

After the Chinese puller left, the Japanese man folded his arms and seemed to think for a while. Then, as if he had made up his mind, he briskly went into the house. What he heard at once was the old woman's shouting, mixed with the crying of a Chinese girl. No sooner had he heard that than he rushed up the dim staircase, taking two or three steps at a stride. Then he began pounding with all his might on the door of the old woman's room.

The door opened at once. But when the Japanese man stepped inside, only the old Indian woman was standing there. The Chinese girl, perhaps hidden in the next room, was nowhere to be seen.

"What do you want?"

The old woman looked him up and down with obvious suspicion.

"You're a fortune-teller, aren't you?"

With arms still folded, the Japanese man glared back at her.

"I am."

"Then you should know my business without asking, shouldn't you? I've come to have my own fortune told."

"And what shall I divine for you?"

The old woman watched him even more suspiciously.

"My employer's daughter disappeared last spring. I want you to find her."

He spoke each phrase with force.

"My employer is the Japanese consul in Hong Kong. His daughter's name is Miss Taeko. I am a student attendant named Endo. Well then? Where is that young lady?"

As Endo said this, he slipped a hand into his coat and drew out a pistol.

"Isn't she somewhere around here? The Hong Kong police investigation said it was likely an Indian who abducted her. Don't hide things if you know what's good for you."

But the old Indian woman showed not the slightest sign of fear. On the contrary, there was even a mocking smile on her lips.

"What are you talking about? I've never even seen such a young lady."

"Liar. The one looking out that window just now was definitely Miss Taeko."

With the pistol in one hand, Endo pointed with the other at the doorway to the next room.

"If you're still going to be stubborn, bring out that Chinese girl who's in there."

"She is my adopted child."

The old woman kept grinning to herself in the same mocking way.

"Whether she's adopted or not, one look will tell. If you won't bring her, I'll go there myself."

As Endo tried to step into the next room, the old Indian woman instantly moved to block the doorway.

"This is my house. I won't let a complete stranger like you go barging into the back rooms."

"Move. If you don't, I'll shoot you."

Endo raised his pistol. No, he tried to raise it. But just then, the old woman let out a cry like a cawing crow, and as if struck by electricity, he dropped the pistol from his hand. Even Endo, who had charged in so boldly, had his nerve broken by this; for a brief moment he looked around in astonishment. But he quickly regained his courage.

"You witch!" he shouted, and leapt at the old woman like a tiger.

But the old woman was no easy opponent. In a flash she dodged aside, grabbed the broom lying there, and swept the refuse on the floor straight at Endo's face as he lunged again. Every bit of that trash turned to sparks, searing his eyes, his mouth, all over his face.

Unable to endure it at last, Endo fled outside, almost tumbling, chased by a whirlwind of sparks.

III

Near midnight that night, Endo stood alone in front of the old woman's house, staring bitterly at the firelight reflected in the upstairs glass window.

"It's maddening. I've finally tracked down where the young lady is, and yet I can't rescue her. Maybe I'd better go to the police and report it..."
