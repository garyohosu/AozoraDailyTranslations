The God Agni

by Akutagawa, Ryunosuke · 2026-03-08

A supernatural suspense tale set in old Shanghai, where a sinister Indian fortune-teller claims to receive prophecies from the fire god Agni and a kidnapped girl waits for rescue.

I

This was in a certain quarter of Shanghai, China. On the second floor of a certain house, dim even in daytime, an evil-looking old Indian woman sat talking earnestly with an American businessman.

'As a matter of fact, I've come again to ask you to tell me my fortune, old lady--'

As he spoke, the American lit a fresh cigarette.

'My fortune? I have decided not to tell fortunes for the time being.'

With a mocking glance, the old woman slowly looked him over.

'These days, even when I go to the trouble of doing it for people, more and more of them scarcely bother to reward me at all.'

'Of course I'll pay you.'

Without the least reluctance, the American tossed a three-hundred-dollar check down in front of her.

'Take this for now. If your divination proves true, I'll reward you again separately--'

The moment the old woman saw the check, her manner changed at once.

'This is so much that I almost feel guilty taking it. And what is it, exactly, that you want me to divine?'

'What I want to know is--'

Still holding the cigarette between his lips, the American smiled slyly.

'When, exactly, a war between Japan and America is going to break out. If we merchants knew that much for certain, we'd make a fortune in no time.'

'Then come tomorrow. I'll have the answer for you by then.'

'All right. Just make sure there's no mistake--'

The old Indian woman drew herself up proudly.

'In fifty years, my divinations have never failed once. After all, it is the god Agni himself who gives the oracle.'

When the American had gone, the old woman went to the doorway of the next room and called out, 'Huilian. Huilian.'

The one who came at her call was a beautiful Chinese girl. But whether from hardship or something else, her full cheeks were the color of wax.

'What have you been dawdling over? Truly, there isn't a more brazen girl anywhere. I suppose you'd been dozing in the kitchen again, hadn't you?'

No matter how much she was scolded, Huilian kept her head bowed and said nothing.

'Listen carefully. Tonight, for the first time in a while, I am going to seek an answer from the god Agni, so be ready.'

The girl raised sorrowful eyes to the old woman's dark face.

'Tonight?'

'Tonight at twelve. Do you understand? Don't forget.'

The old Indian woman lifted a finger threateningly.

'And if you make me trouble again like you did last time, then this time you won't leave here alive. If I wanted to kill you, it would be easier than wringing the neck of a chick--'

But here the old woman suddenly frowned. She had happened to notice what the other girl was doing: Huilian had gone over to the window and was gazing out at the lonely street through the open glass pane.

'What are you looking at?'

Huilian turned even paler and looked up once more at the old woman's face.

'Very well, very well. If you're going to make a fool of me like that, then you still haven't had enough pain.'

Her eyes blazing, the old woman snatched up the broom beside her.

At that exact moment, someone apparently came to the house, for there suddenly began a violent pounding at the door.

II

At about the same time that day, a young Japanese man happened to pass in front of that house. For some reason, the moment he caught sight of the Chinese girl leaning out from the upstairs window, he stopped dead and stood there staring in blank amazement.

Then an elderly Chinese rickshaw puller happened to pass by as well.

'Hey. Hey. Do you know who lives up there on the second floor?'

The Japanese man abruptly put the question to him. Still gripping the shafts of his rickshaw, the Chinese man looked up at the high window.

'There? Some old Indian woman lives there, so I've heard,' he answered uneasily, as though about to hurry on.

'Wait a moment. And what is it that old woman does for a living?'

'A fortune-teller. But around here they say she even practices magic. If you value your life, you'd do better not to go anywhere near that old woman's place.'

After the rickshaw puller had gone, the Japanese man stood with his arms folded, as though thinking something over. Then, as if he had made up his mind, he strode straight into the house. Almost at once he heard the old woman's shouting, mingled with the crying of the Chinese girl. The moment he heard it, he raced up the dark stairway, taking two or three steps at a stride. Then he began pounding with all his strength on the door of the old woman's room.

The door opened at once. But when the Japanese man stepped inside, he found only the old Indian woman standing there by herself. The Chinese girl, perhaps hidden away in the next room, was nowhere to be seen.

'What do you want?'

The old woman looked him up and down with the utmost suspicion.

'You're a fortune-teller, aren't you?'

With his arms still folded, the Japanese man glared back at her.

'I am.'

'Then you ought to know what I want without my having to tell you. I've come to have you tell me one thing as well.'

'And what is it you want me to divine?'

The old woman watched him all the more suspiciously.

'My employer's daughter disappeared last spring. I want you to tell me where she is--'

He spoke each phrase with deliberate force.

'My employer is the Japanese consul in Hong Kong. His daughter's name is Miss Taeko. I am a student named Endo. Well then? Where is that young lady now?'

As he said this, Endo slipped a hand inside his coat and drew out a pistol.

'Isn't she somewhere around this neighborhood? The Hong Kong police concluded that the one who abducted her was apparently an Indian. If you conceal anything, it won't go well for you.'

Yet the old Indian woman did not look afraid in the least. On the contrary, a mocking smile even rose to her lips.

'What nonsense are you talking? I have never even laid eyes on any such young lady.'

'Liar. The one who was looking out of that window just now was certainly Miss Taeko.'

Holding the pistol in one hand, Endo pointed with the other toward the doorway of the next room.

'If you still mean to be stubborn, bring out the Chinese girl in there.'

'That child is my adopted daughter.'

Still the old woman went on grinning to herself in derision.

'Adopted daughter or not, one look will settle it. If you won't bring her out, then I'll go in and see for myself.'

As Endo tried to step into the next room, the old Indian woman instantly moved to block the doorway.

'This is my house. I won't have a stranger like you barging into the back rooms.'

'Move. If you don't, I'll shoot you dead.'

Endo raised the pistol. No, he tried to raise it. But at that instant the old woman gave a cry like the caw of a crow, and the pistol dropped from his hand as if he had been struck by an electric shock. Bold as Endo was, even he had his nerve broken by that. For a moment he stood there looking around in bewilderment. But he quickly recovered his courage.

'You witch,' he shouted, and sprang at the old woman like a tiger.

But the old woman was no easy opponent. She slipped aside in an instant, snatched up the broom that was there, and swept the dust and rubbish from the floor straight into Endo's face as he lunged at her again. At once every speck of it turned into sparks, burning themselves into his eyes, his mouth, all over his face.

At last Endo could bear it no longer. Driven by that whirlwind of sparks, he fled outside as though tumbling headlong.

III

Near midnight that night, Endo stood alone in front of the old woman's house, staring in frustration at the firelight reflected in the upstairs window.

'To have finally discovered where Miss Taeko is, and yet be unable to get her back, is maddening. Should I go to the police? No, no. I've had more than enough of how uselessly soft the Chinese police are, back in Hong Kong. And if she got away again this time, finding her all over again would be no easy matter. But against that witch, even a pistol is useless...'

While Endo was thinking this over, a scrap of paper suddenly came fluttering down from the high upstairs window.

'What's this? A scrap of paper fell down. Could it be a letter from the young lady?'

Murmuring this, Endo picked it up, then carefully drew out the pocket flashlight he had concealed on him and shone its round beam across the paper. Sure enough, there on it were faint pencil marks that could only have been written by Taeko.

'Mr. Endo. The old woman in this house is a terrible sorceress. Sometimes in the middle of the night she makes the Indian god called Agni possess my body. While that god is possessing me, I become as if I were dead. So I do not know what may happen, but the old woman says that the god Agni borrows my mouth and uses it to make all kinds of prophecies. Tonight too, at twelve, the old woman is going to make the god Agni possess me again. Usually, before I know it, I grow faint and lose consciousness, but tonight, before that happens, I will deliberately pretend that the spell has taken hold of me. Then I will tell her that if she does not send me back to my father, the god Agni will take her life. The old woman fears the god Agni more than anything, so if she hears that, I think she is sure to send me back. Please come once more to the old woman's house tomorrow morning. Aside from this plan, there is no way for me to escape from her hands. Goodbye.'

When Endo had finished reading the letter, he took out his watch and looked at it. It was five minutes to twelve.

'It's almost time. But that woman's a sorceress, and Miss Taeko is still just a child, so unless luck is very much with her--'

Before Endo could finish the sentence, the magic must already have begun. The upstairs window, bright until that moment, suddenly went completely dark. At the same time, a strange scent of incense drifted quietly from somewhere, so strong that it seemed to sink into the paving stones of the street.

IV

At that moment the old Indian woman, in the upstairs room with the lamp extinguished, sat chanting spells over and over with a book of magic spread open on the table before her. In the light of the fire in the censer, only the letters on the page floated up faintly in the darkness.

Before the old woman sat anxious Huilian, no, Taeko dressed in Chinese clothes, motionless in a chair. Had the letter she dropped from the window safely reached Mr. Endo's hands? The figure she had seen in the street at that time had surely been Mr. Endo, and yet what if she had been mistaken? The thought made it impossible for her to sit still. But if, by some careless sign, the old woman noticed what she was thinking, then her plan to escape from this dreadful sorceress's house would at once be seen through. So Taeko clasped her trembling hands with all her might and waited desperately for the moment to come when, just as she had planned in advance, she was to pretend that the god Agni had possessed her.

Once the old woman had finished her chant, she began circling Taeko and making all manner of gestures. At one moment she stood in front of her and raised both hands out to either side. At another she came behind her and held her hands softly over Taeko's forehead, as though she were about to blindfold her. If someone had seen the old woman's movements from outside the room just then, it would surely have looked as though some great bat were wheeling through the pale light of the censer flame.

Meanwhile, as always, drowsiness gradually began to steal over Taeko. But if she fell asleep here, then the carefully laid plan would come to nothing. And if that failed, then of course she would never be able to return to her father again.

'Gods of Japan, please protect me so that I do not fall asleep. And if only once more, even for a moment, I may see my father's face again, then I am willing to die at once. Gods of Japan, please lend me your power so that I may deceive the old woman.'

Again and again Taeko went on praying fervently in her heart. But the drowsiness only grew stronger. At the same time, she began to hear faintly an uncanny sound like music, as if a gong were being struck. This was always the sound that was heard whenever the god Agni came down from the sky.

Once it had come to this, she could not remain awake however hard she tried. Indeed, even the censer flame before her eyes and the figure of the old Indian woman were disappearing before her, just as a sinister dream fades away.

'God Agni, God Agni, please hear what I have to say.'

By the time the sorceress, prostrate upon the floor, raised her hoarse voice in prayer, Taeko had already fallen fast asleep in her chair, as though she knew no longer whether she lived or died.

V

Taeko, and of course the old woman as well, must have believed that no human eye would ever witness this ritual. In fact, however, there was another man outside the room, peering in through the keyhole. Who was it? Needless to say, it was Endo.

After reading Taeko's letter, Endo had at first thought he might remain standing out in the street and wait for dawn. But when he thought of the danger the young lady was in, he could not remain still. So, like a thief, he stole quietly into the house and came at once to the upstairs doorway, where he had been peeping in from the keyhole ever since.

But because he was peering through a keyhole, all he could just barely see straight ahead was Taeko's corpse-like face, bathed in the pale light of the censer flame. The desk, the magic book, the old woman prostrate on the floor, none of these entered Endo's sight at all. But the old woman's hoarse voice came through with perfect clarity.

'God Agni, God Agni, please hear what I have to say.'

The moment the old woman said this, Taeko, who had been sitting there as if not even breathing, suddenly began to speak with her eyes still shut. And what was more, the voice was nothing like that of a young girl such as Taeko. It was the rough voice of a man.

'No. I will not grant your request. You have disobeyed my commands and done nothing but evil. As of tonight, I have decided to cast you off. More than that, I have decided to punish you for your wickedness.'

The old woman must have been dumbfounded. For a little while she made no answer at all, only gasping sounds. But Taeko paid her no heed and continued to speak with solemn gravity.

'You stole this girl from the hands of her grieving father. If you value your life, then instead of waiting until tomorrow, return this girl at once, tonight.'

Keeping his eye to the keyhole, Endo waited for the old woman's answer. But contrary to his expectation that she would be frightened, she suddenly strode up in front of Taeko, letting out a hateful laugh.

'Enough of trying to make a fool of me. What do you take me for? I'm not so senile yet that you can deceive me. Send you back to your father at once? Agni is no policeman. He would never command any such thing.'

The old woman, from somewhere, had produced a knife, and now she thrust it right before Taeko's closed eyes.

'Come, confess honestly. Haven't you dared to imitate the very voice of the god Agni?'

Though he had been watching the whole time, Endo naturally had no way of knowing that Taeko was truly asleep. So when he saw this, his heart leapt, thinking the plan had been exposed. But Taeko, without so much as stirring an eyelid, answered in a tone of scorn.

'Your death is near. Does my voice sound to you like the voice of a human being? My voice may be low, but it is the voice of the flame that burns in heaven. And you cannot tell? If not, then do as you please. I ask you only this: will you send this girl back at once, or will you defy my command?'

The old woman seemed to hesitate for a moment. But almost immediately she recovered her nerve. Waving the knife in one hand, she seized Taeko by the hair with the other and dragged her toward herself.

'You wretched little brat. So you still mean to keep up this stubborn act? Very well, then. Just as promised, I'll take your life in a single stroke.'

The old woman raised the knife. If even one minute more passed, Taeko's life would be lost. Endo instantly sprang up and tried by brute force to wrench open the locked door at the entrance. But the door would not give easily. No matter how hard he pushed or pounded on it, all he did was strip the skin from his hands.

VI

Then, from inside the room, a great cry suddenly rang out through the darkness. After that came what sounded like the thud of a body falling to the floor. Almost mad with panic, Endo shouted Taeko's name again and again, gathering all the strength of his body into his shoulder as he hurled himself against the door.

There came the sound of wood splitting, the sound of the lock flying loose, and at last the door gave way. But inside the room itself, there was only the pale flame still flickering in the censer. Otherwise it was hushed as though no one were there.

Guided by that light, Endo looked around fearfully.

The first thing he saw was Taeko, still sitting motionless in the chair like a corpse. Yet for some reason, to Endo she gave the solemn impression of someone with a halo shining about her head.

'Miss Taeko. Miss Taeko.'

Going to the chair, Endo put his mouth close to her ear and called out desperately. But Taeko kept her eyes shut and said nothing.

'Miss Taeko. Pull yourself together. It's Endo.'

At last, as though waking from a dream, Taeko opened her eyes a little.

'Mr. Endo?'

'Yes. It's Endo. Everything is all right now, so don't be afraid. Come, we must get away quickly.'

Still half dreaming, Taeko spoke in a faint voice.

'The plan failed. I fell asleep, so... please forgive me.'

'The plan being exposed was not your fault. Didn't you carry through exactly as we arranged and pretend that Agni had possessed you? Never mind that now. Come, you must escape at once.'

Impatiently, Endo lifted Taeko up from the chair.

'But that's not true. I fell asleep. I don't know what I said.'

Leaning against Endo's chest, Taeko murmured softly,

'The plan failed. There's no way I can get away.'

'Of course there is. Come with me. If we fail again this time, it will be disastrous.'

'But the old woman is there, isn't she?'

'The old woman?'

Once more Endo looked around the room. The magic book still lay open on the desk just as before. And stretched on her back beneath it was the old Indian woman. Astonishingly enough, she lay dead in a pool of blood, with her own knife buried in her own chest.

'What happened to the old woman?'

'She's dead.'

Looking up at Endo, Taeko drew her beautiful brows together.

'I had no idea. Mr. Endo... was it you? Was it you who killed her?'

Endo shifted his gaze from the old woman's corpse to Taeko's face. Only at that moment did he finally understand the mystery of fate's strange workings: that the plan had failed, yet because of that the old woman had died, and Taeko had been safely won back.

'I did not kill her. The one who killed that old woman was the god Agni, who came here tonight.'

Still holding Taeko in his arms, Endo whispered this with solemn gravity.

(December 1920)
