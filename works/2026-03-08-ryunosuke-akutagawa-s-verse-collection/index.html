Purple Velvet

Softly, as if stroking Deep-violet velvet— So spring evening fades.

Busily, the swallows too have come back; Warm spring rain wets the mail coach.

The Gifu lanterns glow a faint red; Spring dusk of “Two Tomoe” (March play at the Meijiza).

Into the red coat Of the stage clown, the smell of dust Faintly sinks; spring is over.

Languorously spring goes down; At the hem of the dancer’s gold gauze, Spring goes down.

Is that the sound of spring seep-water? Or the distant drumbeat Of a dancing girl? (Kyoto travel mood)

My one-sided love, my lonely life— Hyacinths begin to scent the air With pale purple.

If one is in love, if one is still young, Could one really weep Even at the scent of roses?

In barley fields, fresh green velvet; Poppy flowers— A May breeze under May sky.

May has come; Forget-me-nots, and my love too, Must now be faintly fragrant.

With cut wheat’s smell, even clouds turn pale yellow; Summer-day love in the shade Of wild roses.

Lighter than a frivolous woman’s thin love, The rabbit-ear iris Begins to smell faint purple.

Paulownia (To Signorina Y. Y.)

Seeing you—how many years have turned? And now again, A day when paulownia blossoms.

Those mornings and evenings we came and went together— How many times we stepped On fallen camellias.

Even the touch of an old Hiroshige print Is hard to forget, Seeing it with you.

Before I knew it, the paulownia flowers taught me The sadness Of my very young days.

A red bird kept by the sickroom window Cries again and again, Making me think of you.

At evening the Atago Hotel lights up, As if to wake again My sorrow.

Behind grass-colored curtains, A lamp is lit; Child who weeps, what do you think of?

Even the smell of medicine turns cold; By the sickroom window A furan flower blooms.

Writing ADIEU in blue chalk on the wall, The child went out; No one knows where.

Do not blame That bohemian child Who vanished without a word after that day.

From far away, the scent of paulownia blooms Comes visiting somehow— What am I to do with it?

(4.9.14)

Roses

To scatter withered roses and send them— That would suit Love’s wake.

From no perfumed bed Is there anything fit to lay In love’s coffin.

A sweet-smelling silk pillow, A rose-colored feather quilt— A careful grave.

When night breaks we will be travelers on the road; So do not cry so much, Woman.

From that night on, she grew sensual, Like a courtesan— Do I only despise it?

If only someone would pour ointment on my feet; If only there were a girl To wipe it with black hair. (Three poems at a temple)

In the dim twilight of my soul, A yellow candle too Burns faintly.

Head bowed, when I walk the white-night city, When the Good Friday bells ring.

A faint musk wind blows on me, As if from a country In the Red-Lantern anthology.

These tears are only momentary, Yet if we weep close together, It is sad like love.

As the pale-yellow bed curtains Sway listlessly, Autumn must have come.

Roses, do not send out so much fragrance; In the thin light before dawn, A woman is crying.

(9.6.14)

Love While Traveling

At early summer dusk on the capital’s avenues, If only I might walk with you again.

Now the sea, in blue wingbeats, Cannot be quietly waiting For night.

At your house, that lantern with long crimson tassels Must now be lighting, Faintly.

How this kind of evening makes one long for the capital; Even the Atago Hotel Must be lighting its lamps.

Even at a far black ship’s light, A young man drops tears, As if in love.

More sorrowful than wandering over countless mountains and rivers Is walking alone Down the city avenue.

Ah, bitter love— A romantic boy Spends all day weeping alone.

Did my sorrow come From the Indian calico sash You wore that evening?

A two-day moon, Fainter even than The nail of your little finger—how moving.

Why lament so much, traveler? Stopping beside the shelves In mandarin orchards.

The lamplight wet with rain, The paving stones too— On the night I saw you off, sorrow deepened.

The water-blue sheen of the gauze summer sash long set aside— As it stays sensuous, Summer passes.

Young People (Sedoka)

How sad these young people of the capital are, Walking the streets to seek the dreams they lost.

Do not even tochi blossoms bloom in secret, As if telling us to remember days when dreams were many?

Are those bluish eyes of a playful woman scarcely real, Or the two-day moon born in the dusk sky?

Quietly one hears a black-haired girl weeping, In that dim light where first love is nowhere to be seen.

Do not answer with such embarrassed reserve, Do not hide behind that long-tasseled crimson fan.

Ah, the two-day moon over dear Ningyocho, The two-day moon that tempts young people’s tears.

At least, if only one had someone one could weep for in love, Like yellow wax burning as it drops tears.

Even steam from the kettle seems quietly pensive, After my friend’s Saikaku-like tales of love. (To K)

Great-river willows, their blossoms ragged and falling; Are they too in love, those great-river willows in water?

Soaked through by rain colder than scented oil, Whose child is that, walking Ginza at twilight?

Young clowns who make a game of love, Do they make a habit of shedding passing tears?

Before I knew it, love too has grown listless; Like transferred perfume (ah, bitter), Growing colder and colder.

Late Sun on Sand

The unreal noon sea, mixed with sand, Dazzles Like mica.

The far-off shore, stretching countless days, Shines in sunlight, As if silver-mud water were smeared on it.

Sparkling—so many tiny motions, Little waves About to vanish into sand.

Little waves, as if newly born, While from high heaven Light touches in a thousand ways.

The halo has no edge in the sky; Below that sky gather The fisher-girls.

The clustered ama women, wholly unashamed; The sky says nothing, Only shines.

As mortal women sleep, The vast sea, glimmering, Seems to hold its breath.

Under solemn light, The breasts of sleeping women Were darkened.

At the limit where little waves glitter, Far and wide, Leaves of kobo barley shine and tremble.

At the limit where bright clouds float, Far and wide, Leaves of kobo barley shine and tremble.

The moment cloud-shadows fall, Deeply, deeply, Kobo barley sinks into blue.

The moment cloud-shadows lift, Far and wide, Leaves of kobo barley shine and tremble.
