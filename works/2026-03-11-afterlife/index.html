Afterlife

by Akutagawa, Ryunosuke · 2026-03-11

In this brief reflective piece, Ryunosuke Akutagawa meditates on literary fame, artistic value, and the fragile hope of being understood by future readers. He rejects the comforting idea that posterity is a perfect judge, arguing that public taste in any age is uncertain and limited. At the same time, he doubts that art can ever embody some timeless, universal beauty untouched by culture or history. Yet beneath his irony and self-mockery lies a deeply human longing: the wish that, even after obscurity and neglect, one unknown reader might someday open his book and find in it a moment of genuine beauty. The essay is both skeptical and tender, balancing despair over oblivion with a stubborn faith in literature's power to reach across time. (QA warning)

I do not expect to find a friend a hundred years from now.

Public opinion is often mistaken, even when it tries to be fair, and still more so when it comes to questions of beauty. History tells us that neither the Athenians of Pericles' age nor the Florentines of the Renaissance were the ideal audience. If the past and the present are any guide, the people of the future will be much the same. Sadly, I have no doubt that the general public a hundred years from now will still be unable to distinguish a grain of gold from a heap of sand.

But even if the public were ideal, absolute beauty cannot exist in the world of art. The eyes I have now are the eyes of today, not of tomorrow; they are Japanese eyes, not Western ones. Why, then, should I believe in a beauty that transcends time and place? Dante's Inferno may still frighten children in the East, yet between the fires of medieval Italy and Japan today there lies a deep mist of six hundred years.

In truth, I am only an ordinary writer. Even if future readers were just in their judgments, and beauty truly universal, my works would never be chosen as classics. So there is no question: I do not expect a friend a hundred years from now.

And yet, there are times when I imagine that after twenty years, fifty years, or a hundred years, no one will know my name at all. One of my books may lie waiting in vain for a reader, buried in dust on a shelf in a secondhand bookstore. Or perhaps the only surviving copy will be so worn and damaged that no one can read it, shut away in the closed stacks of some library. But...

Still, I think: but.

What if someone should happen to come across one of my books and read a story, or even only a few lines? I allow myself the faintest hope that in that story, or in those few lines, my unknown reader of the future might glimpse some trace of beauty.

I do not expect a friend a hundred years from now. I know my dream is at odds with my understanding.

Even so, I cannot help imagining that after a hundred years of solitude, some reader will take one of my books into their hands, and that the images I created will rise, however dimly, in their mind.

I know the wise will laugh at me for a fool. In truth, no one despises me more than I do myself. Even as I mock my own foolishness, I cannot help feeling tenderness for this fanciful hope. Or rather, I cannot help recognizing in it the common weakness of all human beings.

(First published July 27, 1919)
