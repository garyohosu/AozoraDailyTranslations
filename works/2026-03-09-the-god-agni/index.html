The God Agni

by Akutagawa, Ryunosuke · 2026-03-09

Opening excerpt from Ryunosuke Akutagawa's "The God Agni": in Shanghai, a sinister Indian fortune-teller, a captive Chinese girl, and a young Japanese investigator are drawn into a supernatural confrontation. (QA warning)

I

In a certain district of Shanghai, China, on the second floor of a house dim even in daytime, an ill-favored old Indian woman was deep in conversation with an American who looked like a trader.

"The truth is, I've come again to ask for a fortune," the American said, lighting a fresh cigarette.

"A fortune? I've decided not to tell fortunes for a while."

The old woman gave him a scornful stare.

"Lately, even when I go to the trouble of reading for people, hardly any of them give proper thanks."

"Of course I'll thank you."

Without hesitation, the American tossed a three-hundred-dollar check in front of her.

"Take this for now. If your reading is right, I'll pay you separately then."

The moment she saw the check, the old woman suddenly became friendly.

"Why, this is too much. I almost feel bad taking it. So then, what exactly do you want me to divine?"

"What I want to know is..."

The American held his cigarette in his mouth and smiled shrewdly.

"When exactly a war between Japan and America will happen. If we know that for sure, we merchants can make a fortune in no time."

"Then come tomorrow. I'll have it divined by then."

"All right. Just make sure it's accurate."

The old Indian woman puffed out her chest proudly.

"In fifty years my divinations have never once missed. After all, the god Agni himself gives the oracle."

After the American left, she went to the doorway of the next room and called out,

"Huilian! Huilian!"

The one who came out was a beautiful Chinese girl. Yet perhaps from hardship, her rounded cheeks were pale as wax.

"What are you dawdling for? Honestly, there's no girl as shameless as you. You were napping in the kitchen again, weren't you?"

No matter how much she was scolded, Huilian kept her head bowed and said nothing.

"Listen carefully. Tonight, for the first time in a while, we'll petition the god Agni. Be ready for it."

The girl raised sorrowful eyes to the old woman's dark face.

"Tonight?"

"Tonight, at midnight. Understand? Don't forget."

The old Indian woman lifted a finger as if threatening her.

"And if, like last time, you make me go through all that trouble again, this time your life is forfeit. If I decide to kill you, it's easier than wringing a chick's neck..."

As she said this, the old woman suddenly frowned. Noticing the girl's gaze, she saw that Huilian had moved to the window and was looking out through an open pane at the lonely street.

"What are you looking at?"

Huilian turned even paler and looked up at the old woman again.

"Fine, fine. If you're going to mock me like that, then you still haven't suffered enough."

Eyes blazing, the old woman snatched up a broom that was there.

At that exact moment, someone seemed to have come outside, and a loud pounding on the door suddenly began.

II

At about that same time that day, a young Japanese man happened to pass in front of the house. For some reason, the instant he caught sight of the Chinese girl at the second-floor window, he stood rooted in place, as if stunned.

Then an elderly Chinese rickshaw puller came by.

"Hey, hey. Do you know who lives on that second floor?"

The Japanese man suddenly called out to him. Still gripping his shafts, the Chinese man looked up at the high second floor.

"There? An old Indian woman lives there, someone-or-other," he answered uneasily, and made as if to hurry on.

"Wait a moment. What does that old woman do for a living?"

"A fortune-teller. But around here they say she even uses magic. If you value your life, you'd better not go near her place."

After the rickshaw puller left, the Japanese man stood with folded arms, apparently thinking. Then, as if he had made up his mind, he strode into the house.

What he heard at once was the old woman cursing, mixed with the crying of a Chinese girl. The moment he heard it, he sprang up the dim staircase, taking two or three steps at a time, and began pounding with all his strength on the old woman's door.

The door opened at once. But when he entered, only the old Indian woman was standing there; the Chinese girl had vanished, perhaps hidden in the next room.

"What do you want?"

The old woman looked him over suspiciously.

"You're a fortune-teller, aren't you?"

Arms still folded, the Japanese man glared back.

"I am."

"Then you should know what I want without asking. I've come to have you tell me a fortune too."

"What shall I divine for you?"

She watched him even more suspiciously.

"My master's daughter disappeared last spring. I want you to find her."

He spoke each phrase with force.

"My master is the Japanese consul in Hong Kong. His daughter's name is Miss Taeko. I am a student aide named Endo. Well? Where is she?"

As Endo spoke, he reached inside his jacket and drew out a pistol.

"Isn't she somewhere around here? Hong Kong police found that the one who kidnapped her was likely an Indian. Don't try to hide things; it won't end well for you."

But the old Indian woman showed not the slightest fear. On the contrary, a mocking smile even rose to her lips.

"What are you talking about? I've never seen any young lady like that in my life."

"Liar. The one just now looking out that window was definitely Miss Taeko."

With the pistol in one hand, Endo pointed with the other toward the doorway of the next room.

"If you still insist on being stubborn, bring out the Chinese girl in there."

"She's my adopted daughter."

The old woman kept grinning to herself, still sneering.

"One look will tell whether she's adopted or not. If you won't bring her, I'll go in there myself."

As Endo stepped toward the next room, the old Indian woman instantly blocked the doorway.

"This is my house. I'm not letting a stranger like you into the inner rooms."

"Move. If you don't, I'll shoot."

Endo raised his pistol. Or rather, he tried to. The instant he did, the old woman let out a cry like a cawing crow, and as if struck by electricity, the pistol dropped from his hand.

Even Endo, worked up as he was, had his nerve crushed by this. For a moment he looked around in bewilderment; then he recovered his courage at once.

"You witch!" he shouted, and leapt at her like a tiger.

But she was no easy opponent. In a flash she slipped aside, snatched up the broom nearby, and swept the garbage from the floor into Endo's face as he lunged again.

Then every bit of that trash turned to sparks, burning into his eyes, his mouth, all over his face.

At last Endo could endure no more. Chased by that whirlwind of sparks, he tumbled out and fled.
