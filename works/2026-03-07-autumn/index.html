Autumn

by Akutagawa, Ryunosuke · 2026-03-07

This excerpt from Ryunosuke Akutagawa’s "Autumn" follows Nobuko, a gifted young woman whose unexpected marriage, strained emotions, and bond with her younger sister reveal the quiet conflicts between duty, love, and self-deception. (QA warning)

I

Ever since her days at the women’s university, Nobuko had carried the reputation of a brilliant young woman. Almost no one doubted that, sooner or later, she would step into the literary world as a writer. Some even went around boasting that while still in school she had already finished a three-hundred-page autobiographical novel. But once she graduated, things proved less simple. There were complicated circumstances: her mother, who had raised both Nobuko and her younger sister Teruko without remarrying, and the obligations that came with that. Nobuko could not simply insist on her own wishes. So before she could begin writing, she was more or less forced, in keeping with social custom, to settle marriage prospects first.

She had a cousin named Shunkichi. At the time he was still enrolled in the literature department at university, and he too seemed intent on eventually joining the ranks of writers. Nobuko and this student cousin had long been close. Once they discovered a shared topic in literature, they seemed to grow closer still. Unlike Nobuko, however, he had no respect whatsoever for the Tolstoyanism fashionable at the time. He was forever tossing out French-style irony and epigrams. His sneering attitude sometimes irritated earnest Nobuko deeply. Yet even as she grew angry, she could not help feeling there was something in his irony and wit she could not dismiss.

So while she was still in school, it was hardly unusual for her to go with him to exhibitions and concerts. Usually her younger sister Teruko came along. The three of them laughed and talked freely on the way there and back. But Teruko alone would sometimes be left outside the circle of conversation. Even so, childlike as ever, she walked peering at parasols and silk shawls in display windows, and did not seem especially resentful of being ignored. Whenever Nobuko noticed this, she would quickly shift the conversation and try at once to include her sister again. Yet the one who forgot Teruko first was always Nobuko herself. Shunkichi, perhaps indifferent to everything, kept tossing out clever jokes and strode along slowly with long steps through the dizzying crowds. ...

To anyone’s eyes, the relationship between Nobuko and her cousin was enough to suggest their eventual marriage. Her classmates envied her or were jealous of her, each in their own way. Those who did not know Shunkichi were, absurdly enough, even worse. Nobuko too, while denying their guesses on one hand, would on the other hand deliberately drop hints that they were true. So by the time they graduated, the image of Nobuko and Shunkichi had been burned into her classmates’ minds side by side, clear as a bride-and-groom photograph.

And yet after graduation, contrary to everyone’s expectations, Nobuko suddenly married a young man from a higher commercial school who had recently begun work at a trading company in Osaka. Two or three days after the ceremony, she left with her new husband for Osaka, where he worked. According to those who went to see her off at Central Station, Nobuko wore her usual bright smile as she gently comforted Teruko, who kept breaking into tears.

All her classmates were bewildered. In their puzzlement was mixed a strangely pleased feeling and another jealous feeling, unlike the old one. Some, trusting Nobuko, attributed everything to her mother’s wishes. Others, doubting her, spread talk that she had changed her heart. But even they knew these interpretations were no more than speculation. Why had she not married Shunkichi? For a while afterward, whenever they gathered, they made a grave topic of this question. Then after a month or two, they forgot Nobuko completely. Naturally, they forgot the rumor of the long novel she had supposedly been going to write as well.

Meanwhile, in a suburb of Osaka, Nobuko built what should have been a happy new household. Their house stood in the quietest pine grove in the area. The smell of pine resin and sunlight—whenever her husband was away, these possessed the new two-story rented house in a vibrant silence. On such lonely afternoons, when her spirits sank for no reason, she would unfailingly open the drawer of her sewing box and unfold a sheet of pink letter paper tucked at the bottom. On it, in fine pen strokes, were words like these:

“Thinking that from today onward I can no longer be with you, Sister, tears pour out without stopping even as I write this. Sister, please, please forgive me. Before your precious sacrifice for me, Teruko does not even know what words she ought to say.

“You accepted this marriage proposal for my sake. Even if you say that is not so, I know it well. That evening when we went together to the Imperial Theatre, you asked me whether I liked Shun-san. Then you said that if I did, you would certainly do all you could and send me to him. By then, you must already have read the letter I had meant to give Shun-san. When that letter disappeared, I truly resented you. (Please forgive me. Even for this alone I know how inexcusable I am.) So that night, even your kind words sounded to me like sarcasm. Surely you have not forgotten that I grew angry and barely gave any sort of reply. But two or three days later, when your own marriage was suddenly decided, I thought I ought to apologize even if it killed me.

“Because you love Shun-san too. (Please do not hide it. I know very well.) If you had not been concerned for me, you surely would have gone to him yourself. Even so, you told me again and again that you felt nothing for Shun-san. And in the end you went through with a marriage your heart did not want. My dear, dear sister. Do you still remember that today I brought my chicken and told it to offer you its greetings before you left for Osaka? I wanted even the chicken I keep to apologize to you along with me. And then Mother, who knew nothing at all, began to cry too.

“Sister, by tomorrow you will be in Osaka. But please, forever, do not cast aside your Teruko. Every morning as I feed the chickens, I think of you and cry where no one can see. ...”

Each time Nobuko read this girlish letter, tears would well up in her eyes. Especially when she remembered Teruko slipping it into her hand just before she boarded the train at Central Station, she felt unbearably tender toward her. But was her marriage truly, as her sister imagined, an entirely sacrificial one? Letting that doubt enter her mind after her tears often spread a heavy gloom through her heart. To avoid that heaviness, she usually let herself sink into gentle sentiment, gazing as the sunlight over the pine grove gradually turned into the yellow of evening.

II

For about three months after the marriage, like all newlywed couples, they too lived happy days.

Her husband was in some ways feminine, and not much of a talker. Whenever he came home from the office each day, he made a point of spending several hours after dinner with Nobuko. As she worked her knitting needles, Nobuko would talk about novels and plays that had recently become the talk of society. At times woven into her conversation was the women’s-college view of life, tinged with Christianity. Her husband, cheeks flushed from his evening drink, would place his half-read evening paper on his lap and listen with interest. But he never once added what might be called an opinion of his own.

They also spent almost every Sunday on outings in Osaka or nearby pleasure spots. Each time she boarded a train or streetcar, Nobuko thought the Kansai people, who had no qualms about eating and drinking anywhere, looked vulgar. That only made her all the more pleased by her husband’s quiet manners, so much more refined. In fact, when his neat figure mingled among such crowds, from his hat, from his suit, even from his red leather lace-up shoes, he seemed to give off a kind of fresh atmosphere, like the scent of toilet soap.
