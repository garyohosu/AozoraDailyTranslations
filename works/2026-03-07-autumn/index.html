Autumn

by Akutagawa, Ryunosuke · 2026-03-07

Ryunosuke Akutagawa's Autumn traces the quiet, shifting inner life of Nobuko, a talented young woman whose promise as a writer is diverted into marriage, domestic routine, and unspoken renunciation. Set against pine groves, train stations, rented suburban houses, and the deepening seasons, the story follows her bond with her younger sister Teruko and her cousin Toshikichi, whose shared literary sensibility once seemed destined to end in marriage. Akutagawa renders jealousy, tenderness, self-deception, and duty with remarkable restraint, allowing the emotional force to gather in pauses, gestures, and remembered words. The result is a subtle psychological portrait of desire displaced rather than extinguished, and of autumn as both season and inward condition.

I

Since her days at the women's university, Nobuko had enjoyed the reputation of being a brilliant young woman. Hardly anyone doubted that sooner or later she would make her way into literary circles as a writer. Some even went around boasting that while still in school she had already completed an autobiographical novel running to more than three hundred manuscript pages. But once she graduated, there were complicated circumstances that made it impossible for her simply to do as she pleased, especially for the sake of her mother, who had remained a widow while raising both Nobuko and her younger sister Teruko, who had not yet even finished girls' school. So before turning to writing, Nobuko found herself obliged, in keeping with the customs of the world, to settle a marriage match first.

She had a cousin named Toshikichi. At the time he was still enrolled in the literature faculty of the university, but he too seemed intent on joining the ranks of writers in the future. Nobuko had long been close to this student cousin. Once they discovered that they shared literature as a common subject, they seemed to grow closer still. Unlike Nobuko, however, he had not the slightest respect for the Tolstoyanism then in fashion. He was forever producing French-style irony and epigrams instead. Toshikichi's mocking attitude sometimes infuriated Nobuko, who was serious about everything. Yet even while she was angry, she could not help feeling that there was something in his irony and wit that she could not dismiss with contempt.

So while she was still in school, it was not uncommon for her to go with him to exhibitions and concerts. Usually, however, her younger sister Teruko came along as well. The three of them laughed and talked freely on the way there and back. Still, Teruko alone was sometimes left outside the circle of conversation. Even so, childlike as ever, she would stroll along peering at parasols and silk shawls in shop windows, and she did not seem especially resentful of being neglected. When Nobuko noticed this, she would always change the subject and bring her sister back into the talk. Yet the first one to forget Teruko was invariably Nobuko herself. Toshikichi, indifferent to everything perhaps, kept tossing out clever jokes as usual while striding slowly, with long steps, through the dizzying crowds of the street. ...

The relationship between Nobuko and her cousin was, of course, enough in anyone's eyes to suggest their eventual marriage. Her classmates variously envied or resented her future. This was even more the case, ridiculous though it was, among those who did not know Toshikichi. Nobuko too, while denying their guesses on the one hand, would on the other hand deliberately hint at their truth. As a result, by the time they graduated, the image of her and Toshikichi together had become fixed in her classmates' minds as clearly as a wedding portrait.

And yet, after graduation, contrary to everyone's expectations, Nobuko suddenly married a young man who had graduated from the Higher Commercial School and had recently taken a position at a trading company in Osaka. Two or three days after the wedding, she left with her new husband for Osaka, where he worked. According to those who went to see her off at Central Station, Nobuko, smiling as brightly as ever, spent the time comforting her sister Teruko, who seemed always on the verge of tears.

Her classmates were all bewildered. Mixed into that bewilderment were a curious sense of pleasure and a sense of jealousy, though of a wholly different sort from before. Some, trusting her, attributed everything to her mother's wishes. Others, suspicious of her, spread the rumor that she had changed her heart. But even they knew that such explanations were nothing more than conjecture. Why had she not married Toshikichi? For some time afterward, whenever they met, they would solemnly make that question their topic. And after a month or two, they forgot Nobuko completely. Naturally, they forgot the rumor of the long novel she had supposedly meant to write as well.

In the meantime, Nobuko established what ought to have been a happy new household in a suburb of Osaka. Their house stood in the quietest pine grove in the neighborhood. The smell of pine resin and the sunlight always filled the new two-story rented house with a vivid silence whenever her husband was away. On such lonely afternoons, when her spirits sometimes sank for no reason, Nobuko would invariably open the drawer of her sewing box and unfold the pink writing paper tucked away at the bottom. Written across it in a fine hand were these words:

“Thinking that from today onward I can no longer be with you, dear sister, even as I write this my tears keep overflowing without end. Dear sister, please, please forgive me. Before such a sacrifice from you, I do not even know what I can possibly say.

“You chose this marriage match for my sake. Even if you say otherwise, I know it well. That night when we went together to the Imperial Theater, you asked me whether I liked Toshi-san. Then you said that if I did, you would certainly do everything you could and see that I married him. By then you must already have read the letter I meant to give Toshi-san. When that letter disappeared, I truly resented you. Please forgive me. Even for that alone I know how terribly in the wrong I was. And so that night, even your kind words sounded almost like sarcasm to me. You surely have not forgotten how angrily I answered you, scarcely answering at all.

“But when, a few days later, your own marriage was suddenly arranged, I felt I ought to apologize to you even if it killed me. You love Toshi-san too. Please do not hide it. I know very well. If you had not been thinking of me, you would certainly have gone to him yourself. And yet you told me again and again that you had no feelings for Toshi-san. In the end you went and married a man you did not truly love. My dear sister, do you still remember how I carried in my chicken today and told it to bid farewell to you before you left for Osaka? I wanted even the chicken I keep to apologize to you along with me. Then Mother, who knew nothing at all about any of this, began to cry too.

“Dear sister, tomorrow you will be gone to Osaka. But please never cast off your Teruko. Every morning as I feed the chickens, I think of you and cry where no one can see me. ...”

Every time Nobuko read this girlish letter, tears welled up in her eyes. Especially when she remembered Teruko slipping it into her hand just before she boarded the train at Central Station, the image was unbearably touching. But was her marriage really, as her sister imagined, an act of pure sacrifice? Whenever that doubt intruded, it spread a heavy gloom through her heart after the tears had passed. To avoid that oppressive feeling, she usually let herself sink motionless into a pleasant sentimentality, watching the sunlight over the pine grove outside gradually turn yellow with evening.

II

For the first two or three months after the marriage, like any newly married couple, they too passed happy days.

Her husband was a taciturn man with something feminine about him. Every day when he came home from the office, he would always spend a few hours with Nobuko after supper. While moving her knitting needles, Nobuko would talk about novels and plays that were much discussed these days. Sometimes woven into her talk were views of life colored by the Christian atmosphere of the women's university. Her husband, cheeks flushed from his evening drink and an unfinished evening paper on his lap, listened with curious interest. Yet he never once added an opinion of his own.

Almost every Sunday they went out to spend a carefree day at some excursion spot in or around Osaka. Each time she rode a train or streetcar, Nobuko found all the Kansai people who thought nothing of eating and drinking anywhere to look vulgar. That only made her all the more pleased by her husband's quiet refinement. Indeed, amid such crowds, his neat appearance, his hat, his suit, even his reddish leather lace-up shoes, seemed to give off a fresh atmosphere like the scent of toilet soap. During the summer holiday, when they went as far as Maiko, this feeling of pride only grew when she compared him with the coworkers of his who happened to be at the same teahouse. And yet her husband seemed surprisingly fond of those vulgar colleagues.

Before long Nobuko remembered the writing she had long since set aside. So she decided to spend an hour or two at her desk, but only while her husband was away. When he heard of it, he said, “So you're finally going to become a woman novelist?” and showed a faint smile around his gentle mouth. But even when she sat at the desk, the pen moved less readily than she had expected. More often than not, she found herself sitting idly with her cheek propped on her hand, listening without realizing it to the cicadas in the blazing summer pine grove.

But as the lingering heat was beginning to give way to early autumn, one morning her husband, on his way out to the office, tried to change his sweat-stained collar. Unfortunately every last collar had gone to the laundry. Fastidious as he usually was, he darkened with displeasure. As he hooked on his suspenders he said, with unusual spite, “This is what comes of doing nothing but writing stories.” Nobuko lowered her eyes in silence and brushed the dust from his coat.

A few days later, one evening, her husband started from an item in the evening paper about the food problem and said that perhaps their monthly expenses might be cut a little more. He even said things like, “You aren't a schoolgirl forever, you know.” Nobuko answered listlessly while embroidering his collar ornament. Then, with unexpected persistence, he went on in the same nagging tone: “Even with that collar piece, wouldn't it actually be cheaper just to buy one?” She became still more unable to speak. In the end her husband, wearing a bored expression, did nothing but read some trade magazine or other. But after the light in the bedroom was switched off, Nobuko, still turned away from him, said in a whisper, “I won't write stories anymore.” Her husband said nothing even then. After a while she repeated the same words, more faintly than before. Not long after that, the sound of her crying escaped her. Her husband scolded her a few words. Even so, her sobbing could still be heard off and on. But before long Nobuko was clinging tightly to him. ...

The next day they were once again the affectionate couple they had been before.

And then there were nights when even after midnight her husband still had not come home from the office. When at last he did return, he reeked of alcohol so badly he could scarcely get his raincoat off by himself. Frowning, Nobuko attentively helped him change. In spite of that, he even tossed out sarcastic remarks with his thick, unsteady tongue. “Since I didn't come home tonight, I suppose your novel got along splendidly.” Words like that came again and again from that almost feminine mouth of his. When she got into bed that night, tears spilled from her eyes before she knew it. If Teruko could see me like this, how she would cry with me. Teruko, Teruko, you're the only one I have to rely on. Repeating this to her sister over and over in her heart, harried by her husband's liquor-scented breathing, Nobuko scarcely slept at all through the night, doing nothing but toss and turn.

But by the next day, they would somehow be reconciled again.

As such things repeated themselves several times, autumn gradually deepened. Before long Nobuko rarely sat at her desk or took up her pen. By then her husband no longer listened to her literary talk with the curiosity he once had. Night after night, with the long brazier between them, they learned to kill time talking about petty household finances. And this subject, at least after his evening drink, seemed to interest her husband most of all. Even so, Nobuko would sometimes glance at his face with sympathy. But knowing nothing, he chewed the beard he had lately let grow and, more cheerfully than usual, would say things like, “Just wait till we have a child,” as though turning it over in his mind.

Around that time, her cousin's name began appearing in the monthly magazines. After her marriage, Nobuko had, as though forgetting him, stopped corresponding with Toshikichi. She learned about him only from her sister's letters: that he had graduated from the literature faculty, that he had started a little coterie magazine, and so on. Nor did she feel any desire to know more than that. But when she saw his stories printed in magazines, the old sense of familiarity came back just the same. As she turned those pages, a smile escaped her more than once. In his fiction too, Toshikichi still wielded his two weapons, mockery and humor, like Miyamoto Musashi. Yet perhaps it was only her imagination, but behind that nimble irony she seemed to detect a lonely, almost reckless note that had not been in her cousin before. At the same time, she could not help feeling vaguely guilty for thinking so.

From then on Nobuko behaved even more gently toward her husband. Across the long brazier on chilly evenings, he always found her face wearing an open, cheerful smile. That face seemed younger than before, and now she was usually made up. As she spread out her sewing, she would talk about their wedding in Tokyo and other memories from that time. Her husband found the minuteness of her recollections both surprising and pleasing. “You remember even that?” he would tease. Whenever he did, Nobuko would answer without words, only with a coaxing look in her eyes. Yet why she remembered it all so well, she herself often found mysterious.

Not long afterward, a letter from her mother informed Nobuko that her sister's betrothal gifts had been exchanged. The letter added that Toshikichi had established a new home in a suburban district on the Yamanote side to receive Teruko there. Nobuko at once wrote a long letter of congratulations to her mother and sister. “Since there is no one here if we both leave, I regret that I shall be unable to attend the ceremony...” As she wrote phrases like this, her pen repeatedly balked, though she could not understand why. Then she would look up and invariably gaze out at the pine grove. Beneath the early winter sky, the pines clustered thick and dark blue-green.

That evening Nobuko and her husband spoke of Teruko's marriage. With his usual faint smile, he listened with amusement as she mimicked her sister's way of talking. Yet she somehow felt as though she were talking to Teruko herself about Teruko. “Well, I suppose it's time for bed,” her husband said two or three hours later, stroking his soft beard as he reluctantly rose from the long brazier. Nobuko, still unable to decide on a gift for her sister, had been tracing characters in the ash with the fire tongs. At that moment she suddenly raised her face and said, “Still, it's strange to think that I am going to have a younger brother-in-law too.” “Well, naturally, since you have a younger sister,” her husband replied. Even then she said nothing, keeping the same thoughtful look in her eyes.

Teruko and Toshikichi held their wedding in mid-December. A little before noon that day, white flakes began drifting down. After eating lunch alone, Nobuko could not get the smell of fish from the meal out of her mouth. “I wonder if it's snowing in Tokyo too.” Thinking this, she sat motionless leaning against the long brazier in the dim dining room. The snow fell harder and harder. But the fishy taste in her mouth obstinately refused to disappear. ...

III

The following autumn, Nobuko at last set foot in Tokyo again, accompanying her husband, who had been sent there on company business. But her husband, burdened with too many matters to attend to in the short time available, found scarcely a single day to take her out anywhere except for the times he dropped by her mother's place. So when she went to visit the suburban home of her sister and brother-in-law, she had to ride alone in a rickshaw from the terminus of a streetcar line on the raw edge of the city.

Their house stood near the point where the town gave way to scallion fields. But all around it, newly built houses that looked like rentals stood in cramped rows. Gates set tight under the eaves, fences braced with stakes, laundry hung out on poles, every house looked the same. The ordinariness of the place disappointed Nobuko a little.

But when she called out for someone to announce her, it was unexpectedly her cousin who came in answer. At the sight of this rare visitor, Toshikichi gave the same cheerful “Ah!” as before. She noticed that at some point he had ceased to wear his old chestnut-burr crop. “It's been a while.” “Come in. Unfortunately I'm alone.” “Where's Teruko? Out?” “She went on an errand. The maid too.” Feeling oddly self-conscious, Nobuko quietly took off her coat, with its showy lining, in a corner of the entryway.

Toshikichi seated her in an eight-mat room that served as both study and parlor. Everywhere one looked, books were piled in disorder. Especially around the small rosewood desk by the shoji, bright with afternoon sun, newspapers, magazines, and manuscript pages lay scattered past all hope of sorting. The only thing in all that room to speak of the presence of a young wife was a new koto leaning against the wall of the alcove. For a while Nobuko could not take her curious eyes off these surroundings.

“I knew from your letter that you were coming, but I didn't think you'd come today.” Lighting a cigarette, Toshikichi looked, after all, genuinely nostalgic. “Well, how is life in Osaka?” “And you, Toshi-san? Happy?” After exchanging a few words, Nobuko too felt the old warmth returning. The awkwardness of nearly two years, during which they had hardly even corresponded, troubled her less than she had expected.

Warming their hands over the same brazier, they talked of many things: Toshikichi's fiction, gossip about mutual acquaintances, comparisons between Tokyo and Osaka. There seemed no end to their subjects. Yet, as if by unspoken agreement, neither of them touched at all on the question of how they were living. That made Nobuko feel all the more strongly that she was talking with her cousin again.

At times, however, silence would come between them. Each time, still smiling, she lowered her eyes to the brazier's ashes. There was in her a faint feeling of waiting for something, scarcely enough to be called waiting. Then, whether deliberately or by chance, Toshikichi would at once find some topic and break it up. Gradually she could not help studying his face. But he calmly drew on his cigarette, and there was no sign that he was putting on any unnatural expression.

Before long Teruko came home. At the sight of her sister she was so delighted she nearly seized both her hands. Nobuko too smiled with her lips, but tears had already risen in her eyes. For a while the two forgot even Toshikichi and asked and answered each other about the lives they had led since the previous year. Teruko in particular, lively and flushed in the cheeks, did not forget to tell her even about the chickens she still kept. Toshikichi, cigarette in his mouth, watched the two of them with satisfaction and grinned as usual.

Then the maid returned. Taking several postcards from her hand, Toshikichi at once turned to the desk beside him and began industriously moving his pen. Teruko looked surprised that the maid too had been out. “Then when you arrived, was no one home?” “No one but Toshi-san.” In answering this, Nobuko felt as though she were forcing herself to sound unconcerned. Without turning around, Toshikichi said, “You should thank your husband. I made that tea too.” Teruko exchanged a glance with her sister and gave a mischievous little laugh. But to her husband she deliberately said nothing.

Before long, Nobuko found herself sitting down to supper with her sister and brother-in-law. According to Teruko, every egg on the table had been laid by the family's chickens. While pouring wine for Nobuko, Toshikichi trotted out one of his socialist-sounding theories: "Human life is sustained by plunder. It starts with little things like these eggs--" Yet of the three people there, no one was more attached to those eggs than Toshikichi himself. Teruko said that was what made it funny, and laughed in a childlike way. Even in the atmosphere of that table, Nobuko could not help remembering the lonely dusk in the sitting room far away among the pine woods.

The talk did not run out even after they had finished off the fruit that followed dinner. Slightly drunk, Toshikichi sat cross-legged under the lamp in the long autumn evening and energetically indulged in his own brand of sophistry. The liveliness of his talk made Nobuko feel young again. Her eyes shining, she said, "Maybe I should start writing stories myself." Instead of answering, her cousin tossed out a maxim of Gourmont's: "Because the Muses are women, only men can make them their captives at will." Nobuko and Teruko joined forces and refused to acknowledge Gourmont's authority. "Then unless you're a woman, you can't become a musician? Apollo is a man, isn't he?" Teruko even said something like that in all seriousness.

In the meantime the night grew late. Nobuko ended up staying the night.

Before bed, Toshikichi slid open one of the storm shutters by the veranda and stepped down into the narrow garden in his nightclothes. Then, without calling to anyone in particular, he said, "Come out for a moment. The moon's lovely." Nobuko alone followed after him, slipping her feet into the garden clogs by the step. With her tabi off, her bare feet felt the chill of the dew.

The moon hung above the top of the gaunt cypress tree in one corner of the garden. Her cousin stood beneath it, looking up at the pale night sky. "The grass has really grown wild," Nobuko said, making her way timidly toward him across the neglected garden. But he kept his eyes on the sky and only murmured, "Perhaps it's the thirteenth-night moon."

After a while of silence, Toshikichi quietly turned and said, "Shall we go look at the chicken coop?" Nobuko nodded without a word. The coop stood in the opposite corner of the garden from the cypress. The two of them walked there slowly, shoulder to shoulder. But inside the mat enclosure there was nothing but a dim blur of light and shadow, thick with the smell of chickens. Peering into the coop, Toshikichi whispered to her, almost as if speaking to himself, "They're asleep." Standing motionless in the grass, Nobuko could not help thinking of the hens, robbed of their eggs by human beings. ...

When they came back from the garden, Teruko was sitting before her husband's desk, absently gazing at the lamp, where a single blue dragonfly was crawling over the shade.

IV

The next morning, dressed in his one good suit, Toshikichi went out to the entryway after breakfast. He was apparently going to visit the grave of a dead friend on the first anniversary of his death. "All right? Wait for me. I'll certainly be back by around noon." Shrugging into his coat, he pressed this on Nobuko. But she only stood there holding his fedora in her delicate hand and smiled without a word.

After seeing her husband off, Teruko invited her sister to sit across from her by the long brazier and attentively served her tea. She still seemed to have all sorts of topics that ought to have been cheerful enough: the woman next door, a visiting reporter, some foreign opera company she and Toshikichi had gone to see, and more besides. But Nobuko was depressed. When she suddenly became aware of herself, she found that all she had been giving were vague, halfhearted replies. In the end even Teruko noticed. Her younger sister peered anxiously into her face and asked, "What is it?" But Nobuko herself did not clearly know what was wrong.

When the clock struck ten, Nobuko raised her listless eyes and said, "Toshi doesn't seem likely to be back anytime soon." Teruko too glanced up at the clock at her sister's words, but answered with unexpected coolness only, "There's still time..." To Nobuko, there seemed to be in those words the complacency of a young wife secure in her husband's love. Once she thought that, her mood could only sink further into gloom.

"You're happy, Teru," Nobuko said, burying her chin in the collar of her kimono as though joking. But she could do nothing about the note of genuine envy that had slipped into her voice. Teruko, however, seemed innocent enough. Still smiling brightly, she pretended to glare at her and said, "Just remember that." Then at once she added, in a coaxing tone, "Why, you're happy too, Sister." The words struck Nobuko sharply.

She lifted her head slightly and asked back, "Do you really think so?" The moment she said it she regretted it. For an instant Teruko made a strange face and met her sister's eyes. In that face too there stirred a regret she could not hide. Nobuko forced a smile. "It's happiness enough just to be thought so."

Silence fell between them. Beneath the ticking of the clock, they sat listening without really listening to the kettle boiling on the brazier.

"But your husband is kind to you, isn't he?" At last Teruko asked this in a small, timid voice. There was plainly a note of pity in it, and at that moment nothing stirred Nobuko's resistance more than pity. She set the newspaper on her lap, lowered her eyes to it, and deliberately said nothing. As in Osaka, the paper carried articles about the rice-price problem.

Before long, in the quiet sitting room, the faint sound of someone crying began to be heard. Looking up from the newspaper, Nobuko saw her sister across the brazier, her sleeve pressed to her face. "You don't have to cry," she said. But even when comforted like that by her sister, Teruko did not easily stop. Feeling a cruel satisfaction, Nobuko sat for a while with her silent gaze fixed on her sister's trembling shoulders. Then, turning toward her as if wary of the maid overhearing, she said in a low voice, "If I've done wrong, I'll apologize. If only you're happy, Teru, that's all I want. It's true. If Toshi loves you--" As she went on, her voice too, moved by her own words, gradually began to grow sentimental. Then suddenly Teruko dropped her sleeve and raised her tear-wet face. To Nobuko's surprise, there was neither grief nor anger in her eyes. There was only an irrepressible jealousy, making her pupils burn. "Then why did you--why did you, even last night--" Before Teruko could finish, she buried her face in her sleeve again and broke into violent sobs. ...

Two or three hours later, Nobuko was being jolted along in a hooded rickshaw, hurrying toward the end of the streetcar line. Of the daylight world outside, all she could see was the square celluloid window cut into the front of the hood. In it, the shabby houses of the outskirts and the tinted treetops of mixed woods drifted slowly, yet without pause, farther and farther backward. If there was anything among them that did not move, it was only the chill autumn sky with thin clouds floating across it.

Her heart was calm. But what governed that calm was nothing other than a lonely resignation. After Teruko's outburst had passed, reconciliation, with fresh tears, had easily returned the two of them to the affectionate sisterhood they had shared before. But the truth was still the truth, and it would not leave Nobuko's heart. When she entrusted herself to the rickshaw without even waiting for her cousin to return, a feeling had already spread spitefully like ice through her chest: that she and her sister had become strangers forever.

Nobuko suddenly raised her eyes. At that moment, in the celluloid window, she saw her cousin coming through the crowded town on foot, a cane tucked under his arm. Her heart wavered. Should she stop the rickshaw? Or let them pass each other like this? Suppressing the pounding of her heart, she spent a little while under the hood doing nothing but hesitating in vain. But the distance between her and Toshikichi closed in no time. Bathed in pale daylight, he walked slowly along the puddled street.

"Toshi--" For an instant the call almost escaped Nobuko's lips. In fact, at that very moment Toshikichi's familiar figure was already right beside her rickshaw. But she hesitated again. In that instant, knowing nothing, he finally passed the hooded rickshaw by. Behind him there remained only the dimly clouded sky, the scattered row of houses, the yellowing tops of the tall trees--nothing but the same outlying town as ever, with scarcely any people in the streets.

"Autumn..."

Beneath the faint chill of the hood, feeling loneliness through her whole body, Nobuko could not help thinking it over and over.

(March 1920)
