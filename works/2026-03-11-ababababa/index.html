Ababababa

by Akutagawa, Ryunosuke · 2026-03-11

This brief, finely observed story by Ryunosuke Akutagawa follows Hokichi, a young schoolteacher, through his repeated visits to a neighborhood shop. What begins as a comic sketch of a curt shopkeeper gradually becomes a subtle portrait of familiarity, curiosity, and emotional projection. Hokichi’s attention shifts to the shopkeeper’s shy young wife, whose awkwardness and innocence stir in him not quite love, but a wistful tenderness. Akutagawa traces these small encounters with remarkable precision, balancing humor, irony, and psychological nuance. The final scene quietly transforms the entire story: the timid young woman has become a mother, and Hokichi is left to confront both the force of ordinary life and the distance between imagination and reality.

Hokichi had known the proprietor of this shop for a long time.

For a long time indeed, perhaps ever since the very day he had been assigned to that naval school. He had happened to step into this shop to buy a box of matches. There was a small display window, and in it, around a model of the battleship Mikasa flying the admiral’s flag, were arranged bottles of curaçao, tins of cocoa, boxes of raisins, and the like. But since a red signboard under the eaves plainly read “Tobacco,” there was of course no reason the place should not sell matches as well. Peering into the shop, he said, “A box of matches, please.”

At the front of the store, behind a tall counter, stood a young one-eyed man, looking bored. When he saw Hokichi’s face, he answered, still holding his abacus upright in his hand and without the slightest smile:

“Please take this. I’m afraid we’re out of matches.”

What he meant by “take this” was the very smallest kind of matchbox, the sort given away with cigarettes.

“I’d feel bad taking it for nothing. Then give me a pack of Asahi.”

“Oh, that’s all right. Please take it.”

“No, really, just give me a pack of Asahi.”

“Please take it. If this will do, there’s no need for you to buy something you don’t want.”

There was no doubt the one-eyed man meant to be kind. But his voice and expression were so extremely unfriendly that it was irritating to accept the thing meekly. On the other hand, to storm out of the store would be a little hard on the man. So Hokichi was forced to place a one-sen copper coin on the counter.

“Then give me two of those matches.”

“Take two, or three if you like. But I can’t take money for them.”

At that moment, fortunately, from behind a poster for Kinsen Cider hanging by the doorway, a shop boy poked out his head. He was a pockmarked boy with a vague, blurry sort of expression.

“Sir, the matches are right here.”

Triumphant inwardly, Hokichi bought a full-sized box of matches. The price was, of course, one sen. But never had matches seemed so beautiful to him as they did then. The trademark, especially, with its sailing ship floating on triangular waves, was handsome enough to frame. After carefully dropping the matchbox into the bottom of his trouser pocket, he walked away from the shop in high satisfaction. ...

After that, for about half a year, Hokichi often stopped at the shop for purchases on his way to and from the school. By now, even with his eyes shut, he could recall the place with perfect clarity. Hanging from the ceiling beam were hams from Kamakura, beyond question. The colored glass in the transom cast green sunlight onto the plastered wall. What lay scattered over the wooden floor was probably advertising for condensed milk. On the pillar straight ahead hung a large calendar beneath the clock. Besides that, there was the battleship Mikasa in the display window, the Kinsen Cider poster, the chairs, the telephone, the bicycle, Scotch whisky, American raisins, Manila cigars, Egyptian cigarettes, smoked herring, canned beef in yamato-ni style; there was hardly anything in the shop he did not know by sight. Most of all he had become sickeningly familiar with the proprietor, standing behind the tall counter with his sour expression on display. No, not merely familiar by sight. He knew how the man coughed, how he gave orders to the shop boy, how, even when someone came to buy a tin of cocoa, he would trouble the customer by saying things like, “Take this one rather than Fry. This is Dutch Droste,” and so on. Every gesture of the proprietor’s was already thoroughly known to him. There was nothing bad about knowing it all so well. But it was undeniably dull. Sometimes, when he came into the shop, Hokichi would think how long he had been living as a teacher. (Though, as I said before, his life as a teacher had not yet even reached one full year!)

Yet change, which governs all things, could hardly fail to visit this shop as well. One early summer morning, Hokichi went in to buy cigarettes. Inside, everything was as usual. The floor had been sprinkled with water, and advertisements for condensed milk were scattered over it just as before. But behind the counter, in place of the one-eyed proprietor, sat a woman with her hair done in Western style. She could not have been more than nineteen. Seen full face, she looked like a cat. Like a pure white cat with no mixed hairs in its coat, squinting in the sunlight. Thinking, Oh? Hokichi walked up to the counter.

“Two packs of Asahi, please.”

“Yes.”

The woman’s reply was shy. Worse still, what she handed him was not Asahi at all. Both packs were Mikasa, with the Rising Sun flag printed on the back of the box. Without thinking, Hokichi shifted his eyes from the cigarettes to the woman’s face. At the same time he imagined long cat whiskers beneath her nose.

“Asahi. This isn’t Asahi.”

“Oh, really it isn’t. I’m terribly sorry.”

The cat, no, the woman, blushed. The change in her expression at that instant was genuinely girlish. Not like a modern young lady, either, but one of those girls in the sentimental Ken’yusha style that had disappeared five or six years before. As he fumbled for small change, Hokichi found himself thinking of Growing Up, of a furoshiki bundle at Otomezaka, of irises, of Ryogoku, of Kaburaki Kiyokata, and various other things. Meanwhile the woman, still bending to peer beneath the counter, was desperately searching for the Asahi.

Then from the back came the familiar one-eyed proprietor. One glance at the Mikasa boxes, and he seemed to understand the situation at once. With the same habitual bitterness on his face as ever, he thrust a hand under the counter and promptly passed two packs of Asahi to Hokichi. Yet in his eyes there stirred, faintly but unmistakably, something like a smile.

“The matches?”

If the woman’s eyes were cat’s eyes, they now held a coaxing softness as if she might begin to purr. Instead of replying, the proprietor gave a slight nod. Instantly the woman placed one of the little complimentary matchboxes on the counter. Then, once again, she smiled shyly.

“I’m so sorry.”

What she was apologizing for was not merely handing him Mikasa instead of Asahi. Looking from one of them to the other, Hokichi became aware that he too had somehow begun to smile.

After that, whenever he went there, the woman was sitting behind the counter. Though by now she no longer wore her hair in Western style. It had been changed into a large traditional marumage coiffure properly covered with a red kerchief. But her manner toward customers remained oddly fresh and innocent as ever. She stumbled in answering them. She made mistakes with the merchandise. On top of that, she blushed now and then. There was not the slightest air of a seasoned shopkeeper’s wife about her. Little by little Hokichi began to feel a certain fondness for her. Not that he had fallen in love. He simply came to feel an easy, affectionate warmth toward her complete lack of worldly experience.

One sweltering late-summer afternoon, on his way home from school, Hokichi stopped at the shop to buy cocoa. As usual, the woman was behind the counter reading something like Kodan Club magazine. Hokichi asked the pockmarked boy whether they had any Van Houten.

“At the moment, this is all we have.”

What the boy handed him was Fry. Hokichi looked around the shop. There, among the canned fruit, was also a tin of Droste with the trademark of a Western nun.

“Isn’t there also some Droste over there?”

The boy merely glanced in that direction and kept the same blank expression.

“Yes, that’s cocoa too.”

“Then it isn’t true that this is all you have, is it?”

“Well, yes, but this is all there is. Madam, this is all the cocoa, isn’t it?”

Hokichi turned toward the woman. Her eyes were narrowed a little, and her face had a lovely green tint to it. There was nothing mysterious about that, of course. It was simply the effect of the afternoon sunlight shining through the colored glass in the transom. With the magazine tucked beneath her elbow, she replied in her usual hesitant way:

“Yes, I think that was all of it.”

“The truth is, insects sometimes breed in this Fry cocoa.”

Hokichi spoke gravely. In reality, he had never encountered cocoa infested with bugs. He merely believed that saying something like this would be an effective way to get them to verify whether they had any Van Houten.

“Some of them get quite big too. About this size, as big as my little finger...”

The woman looked somewhat alarmed and stretched half her body across the counter.

“Might there still be some over there? Ah, and inside the cupboard behind that?”

“It’s all red tins. The ones here too.”

“Then what about over here?”

The woman slipped on her Azuma clogs and came anxiously out into the shop to look. The absent-minded boy had no choice but to peer among the cans as well. After lighting a cigarette, Hokichi went on talking in a deliberate, reflective tone, as if spurring them on.

“If you give people cocoa full of insects, children can get stomachaches, you know. (He happened to be living entirely alone in a rented room at some summer resort.) And not just children. My wife once had a terrible time from it too. (He had, of course, never had a wife.) Anyway, you can’t be too careful. ...”

Hokichi suddenly stopped speaking. The woman, drying her hands on her apron, was looking at him in confusion.

“It doesn’t seem to be here after all.”

Her eyes were timid and unsteady. At the corners of her mouth she was forcing a smile. Most comical of all, even her nose was beaded with tiny drops of sweat. The instant his eyes met hers, Hokichi suddenly felt a devil enter him. This woman was, so to speak, a sensitive plant: if one merely applied the proper stimulus, she was bound to respond exactly as he expected. And the stimulus would be simple. It would be enough just to stare fixedly at her face. Or perhaps only to touch her fingertips. She would surely take the suggestion he imposed on her through that stimulus. What she would do with the suggestion was, naturally, still unknown. But if she did not happen to resist... No, it is all very well to keep a cat. But to sell one’s soul to the devil for the sake of a woman who resembles a cat is something that gives one pause. Together with the half-smoked cigarette, Hokichi cast out the devil that had possessed him. Caught off guard, the devil must have somersaulted straight into the shop boy’s nostrils, for no sooner had the boy drawn in his neck than he let out a tremendous sneeze.

“Well, nothing to be done. Give me one tin of Droste.”

Still wearing a wry smile, Hokichi dug in his pocket for small change.

Even afterward he repeated the same sort of exchanges with the woman many times. But happily, aside from that one occasion, he had no memory of ever again being possessed by a devil. Indeed, once, in some accidental way, he even felt an angel come instead.

One afternoon late in autumn, Hokichi bought cigarettes and then borrowed the shop’s telephone. The proprietor, out in front where the sun fell, was pumping air as he worked on repairing a bicycle. The boy, apparently, was out on an errand that day. The woman, as always, was at the counter sorting receipts or something of the kind. A scene like this in a shop is never unpleasant to look at. It overflows with a quiet happiness, somehow like a Dutch genre painting. Standing immediately behind the woman with the receiver to his ear, Hokichi found himself thinking of a favorite photogravure reproduction he owned, a picture by De Hooghe.

But no matter how long he waited, the call did not seem to go through. Worse, after repeating “What number?” once or twice, the operator had fallen completely silent. Hokichi rang and rang. But all the receiver gave back to his ear was an indistinct muttering. At this point it was no time to be thinking about De Hooghe. Hokichi first pulled from his pocket Spargo’s The ABC of Socialism. Fortunately the telephone came with a little shelf-like box around the lid. Setting the book on it, he let his eyes pick up the print while his hand continued to ring the bell as slowly and stubbornly as possible. This was one of his tactics against lazy operators. Once, when he had gone into an automatic public telephone in Owari-cho, Ginza, he had similarly kept ringing and ringing until at last he had finished reading all of Sahashi Jingoro. Today too, until the operator came on the line, he intended absolutely not to stop ringing.

After a furious battle with the operator, it was about twenty minutes later before he finally finished the call. Hokichi turned toward the counter behind him to offer his thanks. But no one was there. The woman had somehow gone over to the shop entrance, where she was talking to the proprietor. He seemed to be continuing his bicycle repair in the autumn sunshine. Hokichi started to walk that way, then suddenly stopped. With her back still turned toward him, the woman was asking the proprietor this:

“Say, a little while ago there was a customer asking for fern coffee. Is there such a thing as fern coffee?”

“Fern coffee?”

The proprietor’s voice was as curt with his wife as it was with customers.

“You must have misheard brown-rice coffee.”

“Brown-rice coffee? Ah, coffee made from brown rice. I thought it sounded strange. Fern is something they sell at the greengrocer’s, isn’t it?”

Hokichi looked at the two figures from behind. At the same time he felt an angel had come again. Surely the angel, soaring somewhere near the ceiling where the hams hung, was bestowing blessings on the two of them, who knew nothing of it. Though, to be fair, the smell of the smoked herring was making it wrinkle its face just a little. Suddenly Hokichi remembered that he had forgotten to buy smoked herring. The herring lay there before his nose, piling up its shabby carcasses.

“Here, would you give me this herring?”

The woman instantly turned around. She had turned just as she realized that fern was something sold by a greengrocer. Naturally she must have thought he had overheard what she had said. Her catlike face lifted its eyes, and then, in an instant, began to flush with embarrassment. As I have said before, Hokichi had often seen her turn red. But never until then had he seen her become quite so scarlet.

“H-herring?”

she asked in a low voice.

“Yes, herring.”

And Hokichi too, on that occasion alone before or after, answered with the utmost gravity.

After that incident, perhaps two months later, during the New Year of the following year, the woman suddenly disappeared completely. It was not for three or five days only. Whenever he went in to buy something, all he found in the shop, with its old stove set up inside, was the familiar one-eyed proprietor sitting there alone, looking bored. Hokichi felt a slight sense of lack. He even indulged in various speculations about why the woman was no longer to be seen. But he did not feel inclined to ask the unfriendly proprietor, “Where is your wife?” In any case, as a matter of fact, with either the proprietor or that shy woman, he had never exchanged anything more than the words, “Please give me such-and-such.”

Meanwhile, even on the winter-withered road, now and then a day or two of warm sunlight began to fall. But the woman did not show her face. Around the proprietor the shop still carried an air of desolation. Little by little Hokichi began to forget her absence. ...

Then, one night at the end of February, after finally getting through an English lecture meeting at the school, Hokichi was passing by the shop in a warm south wind, with no particular intention of buying anything. Inside the lit shop, bottles of Western liquor and canned goods were arrayed brilliantly. There was nothing strange in that, of course. But when he looked again, he noticed that in front of the shop stood a woman, holding a baby in both arms and chattering some nonsense to it. In the broad light streaming out from the shop onto the street, Hokichi at once recognized who this young mother was.

“Abababababa, baa!”

She paced back and forth before the shop, merrily amusing the baby. As she lifted it in her arms, she happened to meet Hokichi’s eyes. Instantly he imagined the hesitation that must pass through her glance. Then he imagined, even in the night, the color rising in her face. But she was perfectly composed. Her eyes wore a quiet smile, and there was no trace of girlish embarrassment in her face. Indeed, after a single unexpected moment, she lowered her eyes to the baby she had lifted and repeated, unashamed even before another person:

“Abababababa, baa!”

Leaving the woman behind him, Hokichi found himself grinning unconsciously. She was no longer that woman. She was one bold mother among many. Once it is for the sake of her child, she becomes one of those fearsome mothers who since ancient times have committed every kind of wickedness. For the woman herself, of course, this change deserved every possible blessing. And yet, to find in place of a girlish young wife a brazen mother... Hokichi kept walking, gazing blankly up at the sky above the houses. In the sky, through the passing south wind, hung a single round spring moon, pale and faintly white. ...

(November 1923)
