The Devil

by Akutagawa, Ryunosuke · 2026-03-10

A lyrical and ironic short piece by Ryunosuke Akutagawa, this excerpt presents a Christian missionary in sixteenth-century Japan confronting a demon whose temptation is inseparable from pity, longing, and grief. The passage blends legend, satire, and psychological insight, turning the figure of the devil into a mirror of human contradiction.

It is said that in the eyes of the padre Organtin, even things invisible to ordinary people could be seen. Above all, they say he could clearly perceive the forms of the demons of hell that come to tempt mankind. Anyone who had looked into Organtin’s blue eyes seems to have believed such stories. At the very least, among the converts who worshipped the Deusu Nyorai at the Southern Barbarian Temple, this was said to be an unquestionable fact.

According to an old manuscript, Organtin once described before Oda Nobunaga the appearance of a demon he had seen in the streets of Kyoto. It was a strange little creature with a human face, bat’s wings, and goat’s legs. Organtin said he had often seen this demon, at times clapping its hands and dancing atop the finial of a pagoda, at times cowering beneath the roof of a four-legged gate, shrinking from the sunlight. Nor was that all. He said he had seen it clinging to the back of a mountain monk, or dangling from the hair of a lady in the inner chambers.

Among all these demons, however, the one that most interests us is surely the one said to have been sitting cross-legged atop the palanquin of a certain princess. The author of the old manuscript takes this story of the demon as an allegory told by Organtin. Nobunaga, he says, had at one time fallen in love with the princess and had tried to force her to submit to his will. But neither the princess nor her parents were pleased to comply with Nobunaga’s desire. So, the manuscript suggests, Organtin borrowed the language of demons for the princess’s sake in order to admonish Nobunaga for his violence. Whether this interpretation is correct or not is, at this point in time, difficult to determine. At the same time, for us it is rather a matter that makes no real difference either way.

One evening, in front of the gate of the Southern Barbarian Temple, Organtin saw a demon sitting on top of that princess’s palanquin. But unlike the others, this demon had a face as beautiful as a jewel. And its folded hands, its bowed head, made it seem as though it were deeply troubled by something.

Organtin grew concerned for the princess. Since she and her parents were devout Christians, he thought it no ordinary matter that she should be possessed by a demon. So the padre stepped up beside the palanquin and, by the sacred power of the cross, seized the demon without difficulty. Then, gripping it by the collar, he dragged it into the sanctuary of the Southern Barbarian Temple.

In the sanctuary, before an image of the Lord Jesus Christ, candle flames burned dimly, smoking. Organtin hauled the demon before it and sternly demanded to know why it had been riding atop the princess’s palanquin.

“I thought I would corrupt that princess. And yet at the same time, I thought I did not want to corrupt her. How could anyone who had seen that pure soul wish to defile it in the fires of hell? I wanted, rather, to make that soul ever purer and more spotless. But the more I thought so, the more I also felt the desire to corrupt her. Lost between those two feelings, I sat on that palanquin, pondering with all my heart our fate. If that had not been so, then before ever seeing your shadow I would surely have vanished into the depths of the earth and escaped this misery. We are always like this. The more we do not want to corrupt something, the more we want to corrupt it. Is there any stranger sorrow than this? Whenever I taste this sorrow, it seems to me that the bright light of heaven I once saw and the darkness of hell I see now become one within my little breast. Please, have pity on me. I am unbearably lonely.”

So spoke the demon with the beautiful face, and it shed tears. ...

The legend in the old manuscript does not tell us in detail what became of this demon. But what does that matter to us? When we read this, it is enough if we feel moved to cry out only this: ...

Organtin, have pity on us as well as on the demon. We too know the same sorrow.

(June 1918)
