Rashomon

by Akutagawa Ryunosuke · 2026-03-05

A full modern-English translation of Akutagawa Ryunosuke's 'Rashomon,' a stark tale set at the ruined gate of a decaying Kyoto and centered on a dismissed servant driven toward a desperate moral choice.

One day, toward evening, a servant stood beneath the Rashomon gate, waiting for the rain to stop.

Under the vast gate there was no one else besides him. Only a single cricket clung to one of the great round pillars, its vermilion lacquer peeling here and there. Since Rashomon stood on Suzaku Avenue, one might have expected a few other people sheltering from the rain as well - women in broad hats, men in soft black caps. Yet there was no one there but him.

The reason was that for the last two or three years Kyoto had been struck one disaster after another: earthquakes, whirlwinds, fires, famine. The city's decline had been beyond ordinary measure. According to old records, people smashed Buddhist images and altar fittings, then piled the wood, still flecked with vermilion and gold or silver leaf, by the roadside and sold it as firewood. With the city in such a state, no one, of course, bothered to repair Rashomon. Foxes and badgers moved in. Thieves made it their haunt. In the end it even became customary to bring unclaimed corpses there and abandon them. So once daylight failed, everyone found the place too eerie to go near.

Instead, crows gathered there from who knew where. In the daytime, if one looked up, one could see countless crows circling the high roof ornaments, crying as they wheeled about. Especially when the sky above the gate turned red at sunset, they stood out clearly, like sesame seeds scattered across it. The crows had come, of course, to peck at the flesh of the corpses above. Today, however, perhaps because it was already late, not a single one was in sight. All that could be seen were white splashes of crow droppings clinging here and there to the stone steps, which were crumbling in places and sprouting long grass from their cracks. The servant sat on the topmost of the seven steps, with the seat of his washed-out indigo jacket spread beneath him, absently watching the rain as he worried at the large pimple on his right cheek.

The author has just written that 'the servant was waiting for the rain to stop.' But even if the rain did stop, the servant had no particular plan for what to do next. Ordinarily he would, of course, have gone back to his master's house. The trouble was that his master had dismissed him four or five days earlier. As already noted, Kyoto at the time was in severe decline. This servant's dismissal after years of service was merely one small aftershock of that decline. So rather than saying that 'the servant was waiting for the rain to stop,' it would be more accurate to say that 'the servant, trapped by the rain, had nowhere to go and stood at a loss.' On top of that, the day's gloomy sky had no little effect on this Heian-era servant's sentimentalism. The rain that had begun in the late afternoon showed no sign of letting up. So the servant, intent above all on somehow managing tomorrow's survival - on somehow making the impossible possible, as it were - had for some time been tracing through his aimless thoughts while half listening to the sound of rain falling on Suzaku Avenue.

The rain wrapped itself around Rashomon, gathering into one vast rushing sound from afar. The twilight lowered the sky more and more, and when he looked up, the gate's roof, with its jutting tiles, seemed to be holding up a mass of heavy, dim clouds.

When there is something that cannot be helped, and one must somehow deal with it, there is no time to choose one's means. If he kept choosing, he would do nothing but starve to death beneath a mud wall or on the roadside. Then he too would be carried up above this gate and thrown away like a dog. If he did not choose - after circling the same track over and over, his thoughts had finally reached that point. Yet no matter how long he lingered over it, this 'if he did not choose' remained only an 'if.' Though he admitted that he could no longer afford to be choosy about means, he still lacked the courage to affirm the conclusion that should naturally follow: 'There is nothing for it but to become a thief.'

He gave a great sneeze, then rose reluctantly to his feet. Kyoto in the evening chill was already cold enough to make one want a brazier. The wind blew through the space between the gate's pillars along with the dusk, without the least restraint. The cricket that had been clinging to the vermilion pillar had already gone somewhere else.

Drawing in his neck, he hunched the shoulders of the indigo jacket he wore over a yellow tunic and looked around the gate. He was searching for a place safe from rain and wind, safe from other people's eyes, where he might sleep the night in some comfort. Then, fortunately, his eye fell on the broad staircase leading up to the gate's upper story, that too painted vermilion. Up there, even if there were anyone at all, they would be nothing but corpses. So, taking care that the long sword hanging at his waist did not slip from its scabbard, he placed his straw-sandaled foot on the lowest step.

Several minutes later, halfway up the broad staircase that led to the upper story of Rashomon, a man crouched like a cat, holding his breath and peering at what was happening above. A light from the upper story faintly lit his right cheek - the cheek with the red, pus-filled pimple among the short whiskers. From the beginning the servant had assumed there could be no one above but the dead. But when he climbed two or three steps, he saw that someone up there had lit a fire and was moving it this way and that. He knew it at once from the muddy yellow light wavering across the cobwebbed ceiling in every corner. On a rainy night like this, anyone carrying a light in the upper story of Rashomon could hardly be an ordinary person.

Stealthily as a gecko, he crept up the steep staircase without a sound and at last crawled to the very top. Flattening his body as much as he could, thrusting his neck out as far as he could, he peered timidly into the room above.

Inside, just as rumor had it, several corpses lay abandoned there. But the firelight reached a smaller area than he had expected, so he could not tell how many there were. He could only dimly make out that some were naked and some still clothed. There seemed, of course, to be both men and women among them. And every corpse lay sprawled across the floor with its mouth open or its hands outstretched, so much like clay dolls that one could almost doubt they had once been living human beings. The faint light touched the raised parts - the shoulders, the chest - and made the hollows darker still, while they all remained forever silent, like mutes.

The stench of decay made him instinctively cover his nose. But in the next instant his hand forgot even to do that. Some powerful emotion had all but robbed him of his sense of smell.

Only then did he notice, for the first time, a living person crouched among the corpses. It was an old woman, monkey-like, short and thin, with a shock of white hair, dressed in a cypress-bark-colored robe. In her right hand she held a burning splinter of pine, and she was staring as though peering into the face of one of the corpses. Judging from the long hair, it was probably a woman's body.

Moved by six parts fear and four parts curiosity, the servant forgot even to breathe for a while. Borrowing the phrase of the old chronicler, he felt every hair on his body stand on end. Then the old woman stuck the pine splinter into a crack between the floorboards. After that she placed both hands on the head of the corpse she had been staring at and began pulling out its long hairs one by one, exactly like a mother monkey picking lice from her child. The hair seemed to come away readily in her fingers.

With each hair that came loose, the fear in the servant's heart gradually ebbed away. At the same time, a fierce hatred of the old woman slowly began to stir within him. No, perhaps to say 'of the old woman' is not quite accurate. Rather, a revulsion against all evil grew stronger with every passing minute. If, at that moment, someone had put to him again the question that had troubled him below the gate - whether to starve or to become a thief - the servant would probably have chosen starvation without the slightest hesitation. So fiercely had his hatred of evil flared up, like the pine splinter the old woman had thrust into the floor.

Of course, the servant had no idea why the old woman was pulling hair from the dead. Rationally speaking, he had no way of deciding whether what she was doing was good or evil. But for him, on this rainy night, on the upper story of Rashomon, the mere act of pulling hair from a corpse was already an unforgivable evil. Naturally, he had long since forgotten that only moments before he himself had been ready to become a thief.

So, bracing both legs, he suddenly sprang up from the staircase. With his hand on the hilt of his sword, he strode forward in great steps toward the old woman. Needless to say, she was startled.

At the first sight of him, she leapt up as though shot from a crossbow.

'You wretch, where do you think you're going?'

Blocking the old woman's path as she stumbled over the corpses in a frantic attempt to escape, the servant shouted this at her. Still she tried to shove past him. He pushed her back. She tried again; he stopped her again. For a while the two grappled in silence among the corpses. But the outcome had been clear from the start. At last the servant seized the old woman by the arm and flung her down. It was an arm of nothing but skin and bone, like a chicken's leg.

'What were you doing? Come on, what were you doing? Speak. If you will not, then this is what you get.'

Shoving the old woman away, he drew his sword halfway from its scabbard in one motion and thrust the white steel before her eyes. But the old woman said nothing. Her hands trembled violently. Breathing in short gasps, her shoulders heaving, she stared with her eyes opened so wide that the eyeballs seemed about to spring from their sockets, stubbornly mute like someone struck dumb. At that sight, the servant became clearly aware for the first time that the old woman's life and death were entirely in his hands. And with that awareness, the hatred that had been blazing inside him cooled without his noticing. What remained was only the calm satisfaction, almost a quiet pride, that comes when a task has been successfully accomplished. Looking down at the old woman, he softened his voice a little and said:

'I am not an officer from the Kebiishi police bureau. I am only a traveler who happened to pass under this gate. So I have no intention of tying you up or doing anything of that kind. All you have to do is tell me what you were doing up here at this hour.'

The old woman opened her already wide eyes wider still and fixed them on the servant's face. She looked at him with sharp eyes, red-rimmed like a bird of prey's. Then her lips, so wrinkled they were almost one with her nose, moved as though chewing something. In her thin throat, the pointed Adam's apple could be seen working. Then, from that throat, there came in broken gasps a voice like the caw of a crow.

'I was pulling this hair... yes, pulling the hair from this woman... I thought I would make it into a wig.'

The servant was disappointed that the old woman's answer was so ordinary. And as that disappointment came, his earlier hatred returned, together with a cold contempt. Perhaps his expression gave this away, because the old woman, still clutching in one hand the long hair she had torn from the corpse's head, began mumbling in a voice like a toad's croak:

'It may indeed be wrong to pull hair from the dead. But most of the dead lying here are the sort of people who can hardly complain if someone does as much. Why, the woman whose hair I just pulled used to cut snakes into pieces about four inches long, dry them, and sell them at the guard station as dried fish. If she had not died of the plague, she might still be out there selling them now. And the dried fish she sold tasted good, they said, so the guards bought it unfailingly to go with their meals. I do not think what that woman did was wrong. If she had not done it, she would have starved. She did it because she had no choice. So I do not think what I was doing just now is wrong either. If I do not do it, I too will starve. And that woman, knowing well enough what it means to have no choice, is sure to forgive me for what I am doing.'

That was, in outline, what the old woman said.

The servant slipped his sword back into its scabbard and, resting his left hand on the hilt, listened coolly to her story. All the while, with his right hand, he absently touched the big red pimple full of pus on his cheek. But as he listened, a certain courage began to rise in him. It was the courage he had lacked below the gate only a little while earlier. And it was courage moving in exactly the opposite direction from the kind that had driven him up here and made him seize the old woman. He was no longer wavering between starving and becoming a thief. In the state of mind he was in now, starvation had been pushed so completely beyond the edge of consciousness that he could scarcely even think of it.

'So that is how it is, is it?'

When the old woman had finished, the servant repeated this in a mocking voice. Then he stepped forward and, suddenly taking his right hand away from his pimple, seized the old woman by the collar and said:

'Then you will not resent me if I strip you. I too have no choice. If I do not do it, I will starve.'

Quick as a flash, he tore the old woman's robe off her. Then, when she tried to cling to his legs, he kicked her roughly back onto the corpses. The mouth of the staircase was only five steps away. Tucking the cypress-bark-colored robe under his arm, the servant dashed down the steep stairs into the depths of the night.

Not long after, the old woman, who had been lying there as still as a dead thing, raised her naked body from among the corpses. Muttering or moaning, she crawled toward the stair opening, guided by the light of the still-burning fire. Then, letting her short white hair hang down, she peered out beneath the gate. Outside there was nothing but the pitch-black night.

The servant had already rushed off into the city of Kyoto, braving the rain, hurrying to commit robbery.

-- September, Year Four --
